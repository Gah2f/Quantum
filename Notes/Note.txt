  Dirac notation (also called bra-ket notation) is a way to represent quantum states in quantum mechanics.
  It was introduced by Paul Dirac and is widely used in quantum computing and quantum mechanics.

              This represents the |0⟩ qubit state in quantum computing.

     Bra ⟨ψ| (Dual Vector)

            It is the Hermitian conjugate (complex conjugate transpose) of a ket.

  Example:

   ⟨𝜓∣=[1 0]
   ⟨ψ∣=[1 0]
               This is the dual vector of |ψ⟩.

Global and Relative Phase in Quantum Mechanics ⚛️
  🔹 Global Phase
      A global phase refers to a constant overall phase factor applied to a quantum state.
      Mathematical Representation:
   If a quantum state |ψ⟩ is multiplied by a complex constant e^{iθ}, where θ is a real number (the phase),
   it represents a global phase: 
    ∣ψ global⟩=eiθ ∣ψ⟩ : Global phase does NOT affect physical measurements. 
    It only changes the direction of the state in a complex plane, but not the outcome of any measurement.
    Think of global phase like shifting the whole image in space. It doesn't change the image itself, just its position.

    🔹 Relative Phase
      The relative phase refers to the difference in phase between the components (basis states) of a quantum superposition. 
       Unlike the global phase, the relative phase affects the outcome of quantum measurements.
   Think of relative phase like two waves. If they are in-phase, they reinforce each other, creating constructive interference. If they are out-of-phase (relative phase of π), 
   they cancel each other out, creating destructive interference.

📖 What are quantum computers? 
Quantum computers are a new kind of computer, not unlike your digital computer. 
They broadly work the same way, taking inputs and producing outputs. 
But how they produce those outputs differs tremendously and allows quantum computers
to solve certain problems — but not all problems — much faster than today's machines, 
with potential application areas including finance, medicine, security, chemistry, and materials science.

📖 What is quantum?
Where does the “quantum” in quantum computing come from? It comes from quantum physics — a new set of rules 
some very small physical systems obey. We’re now able to exploit these rules to perform calculations in ways 
that are different to the way computers do them today. These new computers will be made of quantum things like 
atoms, molecules, and delicately engineered materials.

📖 What is simulation? 

A simulation is basically a way to imitate or model a real-world process, system, or phenomenon using a computer, mathematical model, or even a physical setup. The goal is to study how something behaves without 
experimenting on the real thing, which might be too expensive, dangerous, or impossible.

📝 Long story short: Simulation = a “what if” experiment done in a model of reality instead of the real thing.

📖 Why simulation?
There are dozens of mathematical problems that quantum computers can solve exponentially faster than conventional computers, but there is only one that seems natural. Luckily, it is also a very important problem — it is simulation!

The modern world works because of simulation, which is the process of testing out ideas in a virtual environment before putting them to work in a real environment. Ideally, this forms the basis of rapid prototyping at low cost, which brings us to computers.

📖 Qubits
Quantum information is richer than conventional information. 
Whereas digital information is measured in bits, quantum information is measured in quantum bits, or qubits. 
The fundamental unit of quantum information is the qubit. A bit of information is either 
0 or 1, But a qubit of information is much more crowded. It can be pictured as a sphere.

Each qubit doubles the memory needed to describe a quantum state classically. 
That’s why quantum computers can do things classical computers can’t—they harness exponential growth in information.

Qubits	Classical bits needed
1	          64
2	          128
3	          256
4	          512
30	        64 × 2³⁰ ≈ 68.7 billion bits
75	        More than all data ever created (~300 zettabytes)

This growth is why simulating many qubits on a classical computer becomes impossible very quickly.

📖 Quantum advantage horizon
In the future, large scale quantum computers will be much faster at solving some problems than classical computers. 
Today, quantum computers can solve many problems, but classical computers are still faster. 
The era we are in now is called NISQ, which stands for noisy intermediate scale quantum. The key word here is noise. We only have small-to-medium sized quantum computers because noise prevents us from building larger ones.

📖 The common myth about quantum computers

People often say:
 “Quantum computers try every solution at once.”
                     or
“Quantum computers solve problems instantly.”

The passage says this is wrong.
Why?
Because although quantum computers use parallel quantum states, you cannot directly read all those answers.

📖 What makes quantum computers powerful

The trick is quantum interference.

Algorithms manipulate the amplitudes so that: wrong answers cancel out correct answers reinforce

Like waves:

wrong answers → destructive interference
correct answers → constructive interference

So when you measure, the correct answer appears with high probability.

📖  Constructing qubits
Building a quantum computer is first of all about constructing a qubit in the real world, with hardware. And if we have a qubit, we can try to assemble many of them into a real quantum computer. In this topic we’ll explore the various physical systems in use to make this a reality!
Quantum physics is largely about energy at the most fundamental scale. It is here where we discovered that energy is quantized, meaning it is observed to be discrete. These discrete values of energy are called levels — the steps of energy any thing can be measured to have.
Understanding the structure of the energy levels of some natural or engineered system is the first step in predicting and controlling its behaviour. The state of lowest possible energy a system can have is called the ground state. All other states the system can be in are called excited states.

If we have two states like this in a system that obeys the rules of quantum physics, we have a qubit! It doesn’t matter if there are more, so long as there are two energy levels with a unique spacing between them, we’re good to go!

📖📖 Trapped Ions (Quantum Computing Notes)

## 1. What is a Trapped Ion?
- An **ion** is an atom that has **lost or gained electrons**.
- This gives the atom a **net electric charge**.
- Because it is charged, we can **trap it using electric fields**.

Example:
- Ytterbium atom → 70 protons, 70 electrons
- Remove 1 electron → Ytterbium ion (+1 charge)

---

## 2. Why Trapping Works
Charged atoms can be controlled using **electric forces**.

Scientists use **ion traps** to:
- Hold atoms in place
- Isolate them from the environment
- Control them precisely

---

## 3. Where the Qubit Comes From
Electrons in atoms can only occupy **discrete energy levels**.

Example:

Higher energy level  → |1⟩  
Lower energy level   → |0⟩  

Two chosen energy levels form a **qubit**.

---

## 4. Controlling the Qubit
We manipulate the ion using:
- **Lasers**
- **Microwaves**
- **RF radiation**

These signals can:
- Flip |0⟩ ↔ |1⟩
- Create superposition
- Perform quantum gates

---

## 5. Measuring the Qubit
When a laser shines on the ion:

- One state **glows**
- The other state **does not glow**

A camera detects the light to determine the qubit state.

Example:

Glow → |1⟩  
No glow → |0⟩  

---

## 6. Advantages
- Very **stable**
- Long **coherence time**
- High **accuracy**

---

## 7. Disadvantages
- Requires complex equipment
- Uses many lasers
- Slower operations

Classical computers → GHz speeds  
Trapped ions → kHz speeds

---

## 8. Key Idea
Quantum information in trapped ions is stored in the **electron's energy levels** of the atom.
Quantum computing using trapped ions relies on both intrinsic electronic states and
extrinsic modes of motion! The way we couple together trapped ions 
involves placing two or more atoms in the same trap and leveraging their quantum coherent motion!

# Neutral Atom Qubits (Quantum Computing Notes)

- A **neutral atom** has **equal numbers of protons and electrons**.
- Therefore it has **no electric charge**.

Example:
Rubidium atom  
- protons = electrons  
- total charge = 0

---

Since neutral atoms have **no charge**, electric traps cannot hold them.

Instead, scientists use **laser traps**.

Multiple laser beams intersect and create **optical wells** where atoms sit.

This structure is often called an **optical lattice**.

---

## 3. Where the Qubit Comes From
Just like trapped ions, atoms have **discrete energy levels**.

Two chosen energy levels form the qubit states:

|0⟩ → lower energy state  
|1⟩ → higher energy state  

These energy levels store **quantum information**.

---

## 4. Controlling the Qubit
Scientists manipulate the atoms using:

- lasers
- microwaves
- RF radiation

These signals can:
- flip |0⟩ ↔ |1⟩
- create superposition
- perform quantum operations

---

## 5. Measuring the Qubit
The atom interacts with light.

One energy state emits light while the other does not.

A **camera or detector** measures the light to determine the qubit state.

---

## 6. Example Atom Used
Rubidium atoms are commonly used because they have:

- one outer electron
- easy-to-control energy levels

---

## 7. Key Difference from Trapped Ions

Trapped Ions
- atoms are **charged**
- trapped using **electric fields**

Neutral Atoms
- atoms are **not charged**
- trapped using **laser light**

---

## 8. Key Idea
Neutral atom quantum computers use **laser traps to hold atoms**, and their **electron energy levels store the qubit states**.

# Color Centers (Quantum Computing Notes)

## 1. What is a Color Center?
A **color center** is a defect inside a crystal.

A crystal normally has atoms arranged in a **perfect repeating pattern**.

A defect can occur when:
- an atom is missing
- a different atom replaces it

These defects can trap **electrons**.

---

## 2. Example: NV Center in Diamond
A common color center is the **NV (Nitrogen-Vacancy) center**.

It forms when:
- one **carbon atom** is replaced by **nitrogen**
- a **neighboring carbon atom is missing**

Structure:

Nitrogen atom + empty spot (vacancy)

This defect traps an **electron**.

---

## 3. Where the Qubit Comes From
The trapped electron has **discrete energy levels**.

Two energy levels are chosen to represent the qubit:

|0⟩ → ground state  
|1⟩ → excited state  

These energy states store **quantum information**.

---

## 4. Controlling the Qubit
Scientists use:

- lasers
- microwaves
- RF radiation

to manipulate the electron's energy state.

These signals can:
- flip |0⟩ ↔ |1⟩
- create superposition
- perform quantum gates

---

## 5. Measuring the Qubit
When light shines on the defect:

- one state emits light
- the other emits less light

Detectors measure this difference to determine the qubit state.

---

## 6. Advantages
- Exists **naturally in solid materials**
- Works at **higher temperatures** than many qubits
- No need to trap atoms

---

## 7. Disadvantages
- Hard to manufacture reliably
- Many systems rely on **naturally occurring defects**

---

## 8. Key Idea
Color center qubits use **electrons trapped in crystal defects** (like NV centers in diamonds) to store quantum information.

# Superposition in Waves

## 1. Superposition Principle
When two waves overlap, the resulting wave is the **sum of the two waves**.

The waves do not affect each other directly; they only combine through the medium.

Result wave = Wave 1 + Wave 2

---

## 2. Constructive Interference
If two identical waves align perfectly:

- Peaks align with peaks
- Troughs align with troughs

Result:
- The amplitude becomes **larger**

Example:
Amplitude doubles.

This is called **constructive interference**.

---

## 3. Destructive Interference
If one wave is shifted (phase shifted) so that:

- Peaks align with troughs

Result:
- The waves **cancel each other**

Amplitude becomes **zero**.

This is called **destructive interference**.

---

## 4. Wave Properties That Control the Result

Three properties determine the final wave pattern:

Amplitude → height of the wave  
Phase → horizontal shift of the wave  
Wavelength → distance between peaks  

By adjusting these, we can create many different patterns.

---

## 5. Why This Matters in Quantum Computing
Quantum algorithms rely on **interference**.

They manipulate wave-like quantum states so that:

Correct answers → constructive interference  
Wrong answers → destructive interference

# Wave Collapse (Polarization & Measurement)

## 1. Key Idea
A **polarizer (fence)** does two things:

1. Forces the wave into its own polarization (vertical)
2. Reduces the amplitude depending on alignment

This is similar to **measurement in quantum mechanics**.

---

## 2. Collapse Effect
After passing through the fence:

- The wave is ALWAYS **vertically polarized**
- Original polarization is **lost**

This is called **collapse**.

---

## 3. Amplitude Behavior

Let θ = angle between incoming wave and vertical direction

Output amplitude:

Amplitude = cos(θ)

---

## 4. Special Cases

θ = 0°   → fully vertical → amplitude = 1 (passes fully)  
θ = 90°  → horizontal     → amplitude = 0 (blocked)  
0° < θ < 90° → partial transmission  

---

## 5. Example

If output amplitude = 1/2:

cos(θ) = 1/2  
θ = 60°  

---

## 6. Interpretation

- More aligned → more passes through  
- Less aligned → less passes through  
- Perpendicular → blocked  

---

## 7. Quantum Connection

This models **quantum measurement**:

- Before measurement → superposition  
- After measurement → definite state  

Wave → collapses to one polarization  
Qubit → collapses to |0⟩ or |1⟩  

---

## 8. Key Idea
A polarizer "measures" the wave and **forces it into a specific state**, reducing amplitude based on alignment.

# Circular Polarization & Superposition

## 1. Basic Polarizations
We use two base states:

V → Vertical polarization  
H → Horizontal polarization  

These act like **basis vectors**.

---

## 2. Linear Superpositions

Diagonal polarization:
D = V + H  

Anti-diagonal polarization:
A = V - H  

These are still **linear polarizations**, just at angles.

---

## 3. Phase Matters
To create more complex states, we add a **phase** between V and H.

General form:

V + e^(iϕ) H

Where:
- ϕ = phase
- i = imaginary unit (i² = -1)

---

## 4. Special Cases (Important!)

ϕ = 0:
V + H → Diagonal (D)

ϕ = π:
V - H → Anti-diagonal (A)

ϕ = π/2:
V + iH → Right-circular (R)

ϕ = -π/2:
V - iH → Left-circular (L)

---

## 5. What Circular Polarization Means
- The wave’s direction is **rotating over time**
- Not fixed like linear polarization
- Creates a **spiral-like motion**

---

## 6. Key Idea
Polarization is not just direction — it also includes **phase**.

Changing phase creates:
- linear polarization (D, A)
- circular polarization (R, L)

# Bloch Sphere (Polarization & Qubits)

## 1. Key Idea
All possible polarization (or qubit) states can be represented on a **sphere**.

- For light → called **Poincaré sphere**
- For qubits → called **Bloch sphere**

---

## 2. Poles (Top & Bottom)

North Pole  → Horizontal (H)  
South Pole  → Vertical (V)  

These are the **pure basis states**.

---

## 3. Equator (Important!)
States on the equator are:

V + e^(iϕ) H

- Same amplitude
- Only differ by **phase (ϕ)**

---

## 4. Special Points on Equator

ϕ = 0       → Diagonal (D) = V + H  
ϕ = π       → Anti-diagonal (A) = V - H  
ϕ = π/2     → Right-circular (R) = V + iH  
ϕ = -π/2    → Left-circular (L) = V - iH  

---

## 5. How to Read the Sphere

- Latitude → balance between V and H (amplitude)
- Longitude → phase (ϕ)

---

## 6. Intuition

Think of it like Earth 🌍:

- Poles → pure states  
- Equator → equal mixtures with different phases  
- Anywhere else → general superposition  

---

## 7. Quantum Meaning

A qubit state:

|ψ⟩ = α|0⟩ + β|1⟩  

is just a point on the Bloch sphere.

---

## 8. Key Insight
Changing:
- amplitude → moves up/down  
- phase → rotates around  

So:
- Rotation around sphere = quantum operations

# Quantum Energy & Superposition

## 1. Key Idea
Quantum physics deals with **energy at the fundamental scale**:

- Energy is **quantized** → occurs in discrete steps

---

## 2. Energy Levels

- **Ground state** → lowest possible energy  
- **Excited states** → higher energy levels  

Understanding energy levels helps predict and control quantum behavior.

## 3. Superposition Principle

If |ψ1⟩ and |ψ2⟩ are valid quantum states:

|ψ⟩ = α|ψ1⟩ + β|ψ2⟩  

is also a valid quantum state.

- α, β = complex amplitudes
- System can exist in **multiple states at once** until measured

---

# Superposition in the Abstract: It’s All Relative

## 1. Key Idea
- Superposition depends on **what states we consider important**.  
- In physical systems, “important” states are often **energy levels** or **polarizations**.  
- This choice is **ours**, not dictated by Nature.

---

## 2. Examples

- Diagonal polarization:
  - D = H + V  
  - We define horizontal (H) and vertical (V) as our **reference states**  
- The choice of H/V is arbitrary; another observer could define different axes.

---

## 3. Implications

- Superposition is **relative to the chosen basis**  
- Changing the basis changes which states are considered “superpositions”

---

## 4. Conceptual Question

- Is horizontal polarization (H) a superposition?  
  - **No**, in the H/V basis, H is a **pure basis state**, not a combination  
  - In a different basis, it **could be a superposition**

---

## 5. Key Insight

Superposition is **basis-dependent**:

- In one basis → pure state  
- In another basis → superposition  

This flexibility is central to **quantum computing**.
 
# Superposition in the Abstract: Arithmetic Analogy

## 1. Key Idea
- Superposition can be treated like **arithmetic on abstract symbols**  
- Anything that supports superposition has **states (symbols)** and **rules** for combining them  
- Adding states → new valid state

---

## 2. Analogy: Mixing Colors

- Subtractive (paint) mixing obeys a superposition principle:  
  - Cyan + Yellow → Green  
  - C + Y = G

- Additive (light) mixing: RGB  
  - Red + Green + Blue → White  
  - R + G + B = White  

---

## 3. Implications for Quantum

- Superposition = **linear combination of basis states**  
- Like arithmetic → you just add components according to the rules  
- Abstracted from physical implementation (energy, polarization, spin, etc.)

---

## 4. Key Insight

- Superposition is **universal and abstract**  
- It doesn’t matter what the symbols represent physically  
- You just combine them according to **linear rules**

# Superposition in the Abstract: Kets

## 1. Key Idea
- A **ket** |ψ⟩ is a **vector in an abstract complex vector space**  
- It represents the **quantum state** of a system

---

## 2. Components

- **Complex numbers**: amplitudes can have **phases** (not just real numbers)  
- **Vector space**: set of objects that can be **added together** and obey simple rules  
- **Vector**: an individual ket |ψ⟩ inside this space

---

## 3. Notation

- |0⟩ → ground state  
- |1⟩ → excited state  
- |ψ⟩ → general quantum state (can be superposition)

Example:

|ψ⟩ = α|0⟩ + β|1⟩  

- α, β = complex amplitudes  
- Describes everything about the quantum object  

---

## 4. Superposition

- If a ket is a **sum of basis states** with nonzero coefficients → it’s a superposition  

Example:

|ψ⟩ = |0⟩ + |1⟩ − |0⟩  
- Simplifies to |ψ⟩ = |1⟩ → **not a superposition** anymore

---

## 5. Key Insight

- Kets = **compact way** to store all information about a quantum state  
- Superposition = **linear combination of kets**

# Computation Lite: Classical Logic Basics

## 1. Key Idea
- Any classical computation can be done with **simple logic functions**  
- The **NAND function** is universal: any computation can be built from it

---

## 2. Logic Functions

### NOT
- Input: 0 → Output: 1, Input: 1 → Output: 0  
- Flips a single bit

### AND
- Input: two bits  
- Output = 1 only if **both inputs = 1**  
- Otherwise output = 0

### NAND
- **AND followed by NOT**  
- Truth table:

| A | B | AND | NAND |

# Superposition of Data

## 1. Key Idea

  |0⟩ → |0⟩ + |1⟩

## 4. Key Insight

- Superposition allows **massive parallelism** in quantum computation  
- The subtlety comes when **measuring** — measurement collapses the superposition to one outcome

# Superposition, super power

## 1. What is Superposition?

- Qubit states that are **not** ∣0⟩ or ∣1⟩ are called **superposition states**
- Superposition allows a **richer set of possibilities** than classical bits
- Mathematically, every superposition state is a combination of ∣0⟩ and ∣1⟩:

\[
|ψ⟩ = α|0⟩ + β|1⟩
\]

- α and β are numbers constrained so the qubit stays on the Bloch sphere

---

## 2. Meaning of α and β

- They indicate **how much of each basis state** (∣0⟩ and ∣1⟩) is present in the superposition
- Infinite possibilities exist due to freedom in choosing α and β

---

## 3. Famous Superposition States

1. **Plus-state (∣+⟩)**

\[
|+⟩ = |0⟩ + |1⟩
\]

- Equally weighted sum of ∣0⟩ and ∣1⟩
- Most common superposition state

2. **Minus-state (∣−⟩)**

\[
|−⟩ = |0⟩ - |1⟩
\]

- Opposite of the plus-state
- ∣+⟩ and ∣−⟩ are **orthogonal** to each other

---

## 4. Key Insight

- Superposition is the **first “superpower”** of qubits over classical bits
- It allows qubits to encode **more information simultaneously** than a single classical bit 

# Tangled in entanglement

## 1. Superposition vs. Entanglement

- **Superposition alone**: Qubit in ∣0⟩ + ∣1⟩
  - Measuring gives a random outcome
  - Not enough for quantum advantage

- **Entanglement**: Superpositions of **correlation**
  - Qubits are no longer independent
  - Only the correlations between qubits fully describe the system
  - Entire system must be treated as a single complex state

---

## 2. Classical Analogy

- Shoes example:
  - Left shoe is on left foot → right shoe on right foot
  - Classical correlation due to a **common cause** (you putting shoes on)
- Quantum entanglement:
  - Correlations exist **without an evident classical cause**
  - Measurement outcomes of entangled qubits are linked even if separated

---

## 3. Importance for Quantum Computation

- Entanglement is **necessary** for quantum algorithms to outperform classical computation
- Enables **exponentially large state spaces** in superposition

---

## 4. Quantitative Expression

- n entangled qubits → require **2ⁿ classical states** to describe the system in superposition
- Example: 3 qubits → 2³ = 8 classical states

# Why a Circle? (Bloch Circle)

## 1. Core Idea

- A qubit state:
  
  |ψ⟩ = α|0⟩ + β|1⟩

- The coefficients α and β are NOT arbitrary
- They must satisfy a constraint

---

## 2. Normalization Condition

- The rule:

  α² + β² = 1

- This ensures:
  - Total probability = 1

---

## 3. Why a Circle?

- Equation α² + β² = 1 is the equation of a circle
- Just like:
  - x² + y² = 1 → circle in math

- So:
  - α → x-axis
  - β → y-axis

- Result:
  - All valid qubit states lie on a circle

---

## 4. Physical Meaning

- α² = probability of measuring ∣0⟩
- β² = probability of measuring ∣1⟩

- Since probabilities must sum to 1:
  
  α² + β² = 1

---

## 5. Example (Plus State)

|+⟩ = (1/√2)|0⟩ + (1/√2)|1⟩

- α = 1/√2
- β = 1/√2

Check:
- (1/√2)² + (1/√2)² = 1

---


## 7. Key Insight

- The "circle" comes from probability conservation
- Every valid qubit state lies on this circle

# Trigonometry for the Win (Bloch Circle)

## 1. Key Connection

We connect angle θ with qubit state:

|ψ⟩ = cos(θ/2)|0⟩ + sin(θ/2)|1⟩

- Automatically satisfies normalization
- Links geometry ↔ probability

---

## 2. Probabilities

- Probability of measuring ∣0⟩:
  
  P(0) = cos²(θ/2)

- Probability of measuring ∣1⟩:
  
  P(1) = sin²(θ/2)

---

## 3. Geometric Meaning

- θ determines position on Bloch circle
- Closer to top → more likely ∣0⟩
- Closer to bottom → more likely ∣1⟩

---

## 4. Apply Question

We want:

P(0) = P(1) = cos²(π/4)

Step 1:
cos(π/4) = 1/√2

Step 2:
cos²(π/4) = 1/2

So:
P(0) = P(1) = 1/2

---

## 5. Solve for θ

We use:

cos²(θ/2) = 1/2

This happens when:

θ/2 = π/4

So:

θ = π/2

---

## 6. Interpretation

- Equal probability → state is halfway
- This corresponds to:
  - ∣+⟩ or ∣−⟩ (depending on direction)

---

## 7. Key Insight

- Trigonometry makes quantum states intuitive
- Angle θ directly controls measurement probabilities

# Quantum Gates (Bloch Circle)
Now, we are not using sliders anymore to change the angle, GATES can do it for us!

## 1. What are Quantum Gates?

- Gates = operations that change qubit states
- Applied step-by-step in an algorithm
- Move the state on the Bloch circle

---

## 2. Identity Gate (I)

- Does nothing
- Leaves state unchanged

---

## 3. NOT Gate (X Gate)

- Flips states:
  - |0⟩ → |1⟩
  - |1⟩ → |0⟩

- Works on superpositions:

  α|0⟩ + β|1⟩ → α|1⟩ + β|0⟩

- On Bloch circle:
  - top ↔ bottom (180° rotation)

---

## 4. Hadamard Gate (H)

- Creates superposition:

  |0⟩ → |+⟩  
  |1⟩ → |−⟩

- Key "superposition generator"

---

## 5. Apply Question

Start with:

|+⟩ = (1/√2)(|0⟩ + |1⟩)

Apply X gate:

X(|+⟩) = (1/√2)(|1⟩ + |0⟩)

Rearrange:

= (1/√2)(|0⟩ + |1⟩)

= |+⟩

---

## 6. Final Answer

- The state remains:

|+⟩

---

## 7. Key Insight

- X swaps |0⟩ and |1⟩
- But |+⟩ is symmetric → unchanged

  |0⟩ → |1⟩

---

## 3. Comparing Gates

- X gate → rotate about x-axis (π)
- Y gate → rotate about y-axis (π)

- Both:
  - flip |0⟩ ↔ |1⟩
  - but via different paths

---

## 4. Partial Rotations

- We can apply fractions of gates:
  - Half Y gate → π/2 (90°)
  - Quarter → π/4, etc.

---

## 5. Apply Question

Step 1:
Start at |0⟩ (top)

Step 2:
Apply full Y gate:
- rotate π → reach |1⟩ (bottom)

Step 3:
Apply half Y gate:
- rotate π/2 further

---

## 6. Final Position

- From bottom → rotate π/2
- This lands on the side of the circle

Final state:
|−⟩ (left side)

---

## 7. Key Insight

- Rotations accumulate
- Total rotation:

  π + π/2 = 3π/2

- This determines final position

 # Gates on Kets – How Quantum Gates Act on Qubits

## 1. Gate Application

- A **quantum gate** is an operation that **changes the state** of a qubit.
- In **ket notation**, a gate acts **to the left** of a ket:
  
  Example: X|0⟩ means apply the X gate to the ∣0⟩ state.

- **Order matters**:  
- Correct: X|0⟩  
- Incorrect: |0⟩X

---

## 2. Common Gates in Ket Notation

| Gate | Action on ∣0⟩ | Action on ∣1⟩ |
|------|----------------|----------------|
| X    | X∣0⟩ = ∣1⟩    | X∣1⟩ = ∣0⟩    |
| H    | H∣0⟩ = ∣+⟩    | H∣1⟩ = ∣−⟩    |

- X gate = quantum NOT, flips the computational basis states.
- H gate = Hadamard, creates **superposition states** from computational states.

---

## 3. Arbitrary Gates

- Symbol **U** denotes an arbitrary quantum gate:

- ∣ψ⟩ = arbitrary qubit state
- U = arbitrary unitary operation
- Represents the **general action** of any gate on any state.

---

## 4. Bloch Circle Representation

- H∣0⟩ = ∣+⟩ is located on the **equator** of the Bloch circle.
- X∣0⟩ = ∣1⟩ moves the qubit from the **north pole** to the **south pole**.
- Gates correspond to **rotations** around axes on the Bloch sphere.

---

## 5. Key Takeaways

1. Gates act **on kets to produce new kets**.  
2. X flips ∣0⟩ ↔ ∣1⟩; H creates superposition ∣0⟩ → ∣+⟩.  
3. Arbitrary gate: U∣ψ⟩ allows general operations.  
4. Visual intuition: think **rotations on the Bloch circle**.

## Chaining Gates

- Gates can be applied in sequence; the gate first applied is on the **right**.  
  Example: `HX |0⟩` means apply X first, then H.

- X gate (NOT):
  - Exchanges |0⟩ and |1⟩  
  - X|0⟩ = |1⟩  
  - X|1⟩ = |0⟩

- H gate (Hadamard):
  - Creates superpositions  
  - H|0⟩ = |+⟩  
  - H|1⟩ = |−⟩

- Chaining example:

- Order matters: `HX ≠ XH`

- A chain like `XHX |0⟩` can be treated as a single gate acting on the qubit.  

- Applying `XHX` to |0⟩:
1. First X: |0⟩ → |1⟩  
2. H: |1⟩ → |−⟩  
3. Last X: |−⟩ → (state depends on superposition rules)

## Ket on It

- Two bits of information have 4 possible states: 00, 01, 10, 11.  
- For two qubits, the **computational basis states** are: |00⟩, |01⟩, |10⟩, |11⟩.  
- An **arbitrary superposition** of two qubits:  
  |ψ⟩ = α|00⟩ + β|01⟩ + γ|10⟩ + δ|11⟩  
  - α, β, γ, δ represent how much each computational state contributes.  
  - |α|², |β|², |γ|², |δ|² are the probabilities of measuring |00⟩, |01⟩, |10⟩, |11⟩ respectively.  
- Extending this, the **quantum state of many qubits** is a superposition of many computational states.  
- Notation |b1 b2⟩ represents the combined state of two bits b1 and b2 as a single two-qubit ket.

## Diving the Bloch Sphere

- The **Bloch sphere** is a full pictorial representation of a qubit.
- It extends the **Bloch circle** (x−z slice) by including **phase**, giving a complete view of the qubit state.
- A qubit on the Bloch sphere is specified by **two angles**:
  - θ (theta) – latitude-like angle
  - ϕ (phi) – longitude-like angle
- Unlike a classical bit (0 or 1), a qubit can exist in a **continuous range of states** on the sphere.
- The angles allow you to locate the qubit on the surface, analogous to GPS coordinates.
- Playing with θ and ϕ lets you explore different qubit states.

## Getting Complex with the Bloch Sphere

- The **Bloch sphere** extends beyond the x−z plane due to the role of **complex numbers**.
- A **complex number** encodes both a distance and an angle:
  - Written as: α = r e^(iϕ)
    - r = magnitude (distance from origin)
    - ϕ = phase angle
- Special property: e^(iϕ) moves **around a circle** without changing magnitude.
- If r = 1, α is purely a **phase**.
- The **equator** of the Bloch sphere (θ = π/2) represents points with **magnitude 1**, specified only by phase ϕ.
- Example:
  - State ∣−⟩ lies on the equator with θ = π/2.
  - Its position is determined by the phase ϕ.

  ## One Basis to Rule Them All

- The general qubit formula using complex numbers on the Bloch sphere:
  - ∣ψ⟩ = cos(θ/2) ∣0⟩ + e^(iϕ) sin(θ/2) ∣1⟩
    - θ = polar angle (from north pole)
    - ϕ = azimuthal phase angle (around equator)

- **Extreme cases:**
  - ϕ = 0 → e^(i0) = 1 → recovers **Bloch circle formula**
  - θ = 0 → sin(0) = 0 → ∣ψ⟩ = ∣0⟩ (north pole)

- **Superposition states with complex phase:**
  - θ = π/2, ϕ = π/2 → ∣+i⟩ = ∣0⟩ + i ∣1⟩
  - Opposing state: ∣−i⟩ = ∣0⟩ − i ∣1⟩

- All qubit states have an opposing partner on the Bloch sphere.
- These states can be labeled on the Bloch sphere according to their **phase**.

## The Measure of All Things

**Core Idea:**  
Measurement collapses any qubit state on the Bloch sphere to a classical computational state, 
with probabilities determined solely by the polar angle θ.

**Key Insights:**  
- Measurement outcomes: ∣0⟩ or ∣1⟩ (computational basis).  
- Probability of observing ∣0⟩:  
  P(0) = cos²(θ/2)  
  - Only θ matters; the azimuthal phase ϕ does **not** affect measurement probabilities.  
- States at the same latitude (same θ) on the Bloch sphere have identical measurement probabilities.  
- Measurement always collapses the qubit to the observed state; subsequent measurements yield the same result unless the qubit is acted on by a gate.

**Long Story Short:**  
The Bloch sphere shows all possible qubit states, but measurement reduces them to classical outcomes. Phase affects the qubit’s location but not the likelihood of observing 0 or 1.
Phase ϕ only changes where the qubit sits around the equator; it doesn’t change its “height” above the Bloch sphere, which determines the likelihood of measuring ∣0⟩ or ∣1⟩. 
Measurement only “sees” the vertical component (cosθ/2), so phase is invisible to it.

## More Gates!

**Core Idea:**  
The Bloch sphere introduces rotations around the z-axis,
 enabling full control of qubit states using a small set of quantum gates.

**Key Insights:**  
- X, Y, Z gates correspond to **π (180°) rotations** about x, y, z axes respectively.  
- Rotations only by π limit the qubit to a few discrete states on the sphere.  
- To reach any point on the Bloch sphere, we need **smaller rotations**.  
- Common universal gate set: {X, Y, H, Z, S, T}.  
- Z-axis rotation gates:
  - Z = π rotation  
  - S = π/2 (90°) rotation  
  - T = π/4 (45°) rotation  
- These gates change the **phase (ϕ)** of the qubit without affecting measurement probabilities directly.

**Long Story Short:**  
The extra dimension of the Bloch sphere allows rotations around the z-axis.
Using gates like Z, S, and T, we can finely control the phase and reach any qubit state.

## My First Quantum Circuit

**Core Idea:**  
A quantum circuit is a visual way to represent a sequence of quantum gates applied to a qubit over time.

**Key Insights:**  
- A **quantum circuit** is a set of instructions (algorithm) for a quantum computer.  
- The qubit is represented by a **wire**.  
- Gates are shown as **boxes** placed on the wire.  
- Time flows **left to right**, meaning gates are applied in that order.  
- The qubit state evolves as each gate is applied.  
- Unless specified, the initial state is ∣0⟩.  
- Example sequence: applying X → Y → Z is drawn left to right as X, then Y, then Z.

**Long Story Short:**  
Quantum circuits let you visualize and organize gate operations. You read them left to right, and each gate transforms the qubit step by step from its initial state.

## Rotations

**Core Idea:**  
Quantum gates are implemented as rotations of the qubit state on the Bloch sphere around different axes.

**Key Insights:**  
- Gates correspond to **rotations on the Bloch sphere**:
  - X → 180° rotation about x-axis  
  - Y → 180° rotation about y-axis  
  - Z → 180° rotation about z-axis  
- Additional rotation gates:
  - S → 90° (π/2) rotation about z-axis  
  - T → 45° (π/4) rotation about z-axis  
- H gate is a **180° rotation about a diagonal axis** between x, y, and z.  
- Gates are not truly instantaneous; they are physically implemented as **continuous rotations**.  
- Smaller rotations can be expressed as fractions of larger ones:
  - S = 50% of Z  
  - T = 25% of Z  
- By combining rotations, any qubit state on the Bloch sphere can be reached.

**Long Story Short:**  
All quantum gates are rotations on the Bloch sphere. By combining rotations around different axes, you can move a qubit to any possible state.

## Of Qubits and Spheres

**Core Idea:**  
Quantum measurement converts quantum information into classical information, collapsing a qubit to one of the computational basis states.

**Key Insights:**  
- Quantum information cannot be directly “read” like classical bits.  
- Measurement outputs **classical bits**, not qubits.  
- A qubit is represented as a point on the **Bloch sphere**, defined by angles θ and ϕ.  
- Classical bit states correspond to:
  - ∣0⟩ → north pole (θ = 0)  
  - ∣1⟩ → south pole (θ = π)  
- Upon measurement, a qubit collapses to either ∣0⟩ or ∣1⟩ regardless of its original position on the sphere.  
- A general qubit state is denoted as ∣ψ⟩.

**Long Story Short:**  
Qubits live on the Bloch sphere, but measurement forces them into classical states (∣0⟩ or ∣1⟩). You never directly observe the quantum state—only its classical outcome.

## Quantum Leap

**Core Idea:**  
Measurement collapses a qubit’s superposition into one of the classical basis states, 
erasing its previous quantum state.

**Key Insights:**  
- A qubit can exist anywhere on the Bloch sphere before measurement.  
- Measurement always returns either the north pole (∣0⟩) or south pole (∣1⟩) state.  
- Superposition states (in-between points on the sphere) are never directly observed.  
- Measurement is discontinuous and instantaneous — called a **quantum leap**.  
- In quantum algorithms, measurement is typically represented by a special symbol and occurs at the end.  
- Physical qubits are manipulated through smooth rotations, but measurement causes an abrupt collapse.

**Long Story Short:**  
Measuring a qubit forces it into a classical state, destroying its superposition — the qubit “jumps” to a pole, and all intermediate information is lost.

## Randomness Rules

**Core Idea:**  
Quantum measurements are probabilistic; the likelihood of collapsing to ∣0⟩ or ∣1⟩ depends on the qubit’s position on the Bloch sphere.

**Key Insights:**  
- Quantum computers follow **probabilistic rules**; outcomes are not always the same.  
- A qubit state closer to the north pole (∣0⟩) has a higher chance of collapsing there.  
- Equator states have **50:50 probability** of collapsing to ∣0⟩ or ∣1⟩.  
- The probability of measuring ∣0⟩ is given by **cos²(θ/2)**, where θ is the polar angle.  
- The phase ϕ does **not affect measurement probabilities**, only interactions during computations.  
- Rotations (like Y gates) can move a qubit to change its measurement probabilities.

**Long Story Short:**  
Where a qubit is on the Bloch sphere dictates the odds of measuring ∣0⟩ or ∣1⟩, making quantum outcomes inherently probabilistic.

## Again and Again

**Core Idea:**  
Single quantum measurements reveal very little, but repeated measurements allow us to statistically infer the qubit’s original state.

**Key Insights:**  
- A **single measurement** gives only one classical outcome (∣0⟩ or ∣1⟩), providing limited information.  
- **Repetition** of preparation and measurement many times (like flipping a coin thousands of times) allows estimation of the qubit’s original state.  
- The **frequency of outcomes** across repeated experiments indicates the most likely pre-measurement location on the Bloch sphere.  
- Example: 5,000 collapses to the north pole in 10,000 trials → qubit was most likely on the equator.  
- Probabilities (like 0.8536) can reveal bias toward poles and help reconstruct the initial qubit preparation.

**Long Story Short:**  
Quantum measurement is destructive, but repeated measurements let us infer the qubit’s original state statistically, like estimating coin bias.

## Measurement by Design

**Core Idea:**  
In quantum computing, measurements are invasive and collapse qubit states, so they must be carefully planned — typically only at the end of a computation.

**Key Insights:**  
- Measuring a qubit **mid-computation** collapses its state, destroying the superposition and introducing apparent randomness.  
- Useful quantum algorithms rely on **large superpositions**, making early measurements disruptive.  
- Engineers **design when and where measurements occur**, usually at the end of the computation, to extract deterministic outcomes.  
- Properly designed algorithms can produce **certain outcomes** (e.g., collapse to the south pole) despite inherent quantum randomness.

**Long Story Short:**  
Quantum measurements collapse qubits, so they are carefully scheduled; with proper design, final measurements can yield predictable results.

## Seeking Certainty

**Core Idea:**  
Quantum algorithms are generally probabilistic, so achieving certainty is impossible; the goal is to maximize the probability of success by carefully choosing **when to measure**.

**Key Insights:**  
- Quantum measurements **collapse states**, so measuring at the wrong moment reduces the chance of success.  
- Most algorithms **succeed with high probability**, not deterministically.  
- Probability of success can be **boosted by repeating quantum operations** before measurement.  
- Algorithm designers must **time the measurement strategically** to maximize the likelihood of the desired outcome.  
- Example: Quantum search increases the probability of finding a marked item compared to classical random guessing.

**Long Story Short:**  
Quantum algorithms can’t guarantee success but can maximize it; 
measuring at the optimal moment is key to higher probability outcomes.

## Quantum Symbols

**Core Idea:**  
Quantum measurement is described using ket notation, where the state determines probabilities and collapses to a computational basis state upon measurement.

**Key Insights:**  
- Measurement reads quantum information but **also changes the state** (collapse).  
- Qubit states are represented using **ket notation**:
  - ∣0⟩, ∣1⟩ (computational basis states)  
  - Superpositions like ∣ψ⟩  
- Measurement always collapses the state to one of the **basis states** ∣0⟩ or ∣1⟩.  
- The probabilities of outcomes are determined by the **state before measurement**.  
- If a qubit is already in ∣0⟩ or ∣1⟩ (θ = 0 or θ = π), measurement **does not change the state**.

**Long Story Short:**  
Quantum states are written using kets, and measurement collapses them to ∣0⟩ or ∣1⟩—unless they are already in those states.

## Probability Amplitudes

**Core Idea:**  
Qubit states are described by complex numbers called probability amplitudes, whose magnitudes determine measurement probabilities.

**Key Insights:**  
- A general qubit state is:
  ∣ψ⟩ = α∣0⟩ + β∣1⟩  
- α and β are **complex numbers** (probability amplitudes).  
- They must satisfy the normalization condition:
  |α|² + |β|² = 1  
- The **magnitude squared** of each amplitude gives measurement probability:
  - P(0) = |α|²  
  - P(1) = |β|²  
- Amplitudes control:
  - Contribution of each basis state  
  - Likelihood of measurement outcomes  
- They are not probabilities themselves, but behave similarly → hence the name **probability amplitudes**.

**Long Story Short:**  
α and β describe a qubit’s state, and their squared magnitudes give the probabilities of measuring ∣0⟩ or ∣1⟩.

## Born to Rule

**Core Idea:**  
The Born rule determines measurement probabilities by squaring the magnitudes of probability amplitudes.

**Key Insights:**  
- A general qubit state:
  ∣ψ⟩ = α∣0⟩ + β∣1⟩  
- Measurement outcomes:
  - P(0) = |α|²  
  - P(1) = |β|²  
- This is known as the **Born rule**.  
- Normalization condition:
  |α|² + |β|² = 1 (total probability = 1)  
- If:
  - α = 0 → state is ∣1⟩ (certain outcome)  
  - β = 0 → state is ∣0⟩ (certain outcome)  
- In Bloch sphere form:
  ∣ψ⟩ = cos(θ/2)∣0⟩ + e^{iϕ} sin(θ/2)∣1⟩  
- Measurement probabilities depend only on **θ**, not on phase **ϕ**.

**Long Story Short:**  
The Born rule says probabilities come from squaring amplitudes, and only the “position” (θ) of the qubit—not its phase—determines measurement outcomes.


## Noisy in Here

**Key Insights:**
- Measurement collapses a qubit’s state. After collapse, no further information about the original state can be obtained.
- Any interaction with the environment can unintentionally collapse qubit states. This is called **noise**.
- States far from classical computational states (poles ∣0⟩ and ∣1⟩) are the most fragile and susceptible to noise.

**Core Idea:**  
- Qubits are delicate; even small environmental interactions can “peek” at them and collapse their superposition.  
- Noise is the main challenge in maintaining quantum information.

**Long Story Short:**  
- Keep qubits as close to “safe zones” (∣0⟩ or ∣1⟩) to minimize noise-induced collapse. Noise is the Achilles heel of quantum computing.

**Analogy:**  
- Qubit = a delicate soap bubble.  
- Poles (∣0⟩/∣1⟩) are sturdy, equator states are fragile. Environmental “gusts” can pop the bubble unexpectedly.



## Collapse Isn’t All Bad

**Key Insights:**
- Quantum information is analog: a qubit can exist anywhere on the Bloch sphere.  
- Noise can randomly disturb a qubit’s state, but measurements collapse it to **computational states** (∣0⟩ or ∣1⟩).  
- This collapse allows **quantum errors to be digitized** and detected, enabling error correction.

**Core Idea:**  
- Collapse turns a fragile, continuous quantum state into discrete outcomes.  
- By restricting errors to a small set of possibilities, we can **identify and correct them** efficiently.

**Long Story Short:**  
- Measurement is not just destructive—it can be a tool to detect and fix errors in quantum computation.  
- Even though qubits are analog, collapse “digitizes” noise, making error correction feasible.

**Analogy:**  
- Think of quantum noise as scribbles on a sheet of paper. Measurement collapses the scribbles to neat checkboxes (∣0⟩ or ∣1⟩), making it clear **what went wrong**.

**Long Story Short:**

**Analogy:**

## Key Concept: Collapse vs. Rotation

**1. Smooth Transitions (Rotations)**
- Applying quantum gates = rotating the qubit on the **Bloch sphere**.
- These are **continuous, deterministic evolutions**.
- The qubit moves through **an infinite number of intermediate states**.
- Governed by the **Schrödinger equation**.

**2. Discontinuous Jumps (Measurement / Collapse)**
- Measuring a qubit = sudden jump from superposition to a **computational basis state** (`|0⟩` or `|1⟩`).
- This is **discontinuous and probabilistic**.
- Not caused by experimental errors; **collapse is fundamental to quantum physics**.

**3. Schizophrenic Nature**
- Quantum states can be **sometimes smooth, sometimes jumpy**.
- Continuous rotations for computation, discontinuous collapse for measurement.

**Long Story Short:**
- **Rotations** = smooth, predictable evolution.  
- **Measurements** = abrupt, random collapse to `|0⟩` or `|1⟩`.  
- Quantum computing requires careful **timing of measurements** to extract useful information.

**Analogy:**  
- Imagine walking along a winding path (rotation).  
- Suddenly, a trapdoor opens and you fall to one of two floors below (measurement collapse). You **cannot predict which floor** until you fall.

---

## What is the Measurement Problem?

**1. Two Rules in Quantum Mechanics**
- **When not measuring:** Qubit evolves **smoothly** according to the Schrödinger equation (quantum information, measured in qubits).
- **When measuring:** Qubit **collapses** discontinuously to a classical state (`|0⟩` or `|1⟩`, measured in bits).

**2. The Problem**
- There is **no simple law** that tells us:
  - When a quantum state stops evolving smoothly.
  - Who or what counts as a “measurer” that causes collapse.
- Transition from quantum → classical is **not fully understood**.

**3. Philosophical Angle**
- Can anything, not just a physicist, cause collapse?  
- Thought experiment: **“Is the Moon there when nobody looks?”** — Einstein.

**Long Story Short:**
- The measurement problem is the **mystery of the quantum-classical boundary**.
- Quantum states evolve continuously until measured, but **we don’t know precisely when or why the collapse occurs**.

**Analogy:**
- A qubit is like a spinning coin in the air (superposition).  
- The coin lands (collapse) only when **some interaction forces it to settle**, but what counts as “forcing it” is unclear.



# The "Real" World
## Is the Quantum State Real?

**Key Question:**
- Is the quantum state `|ψ⟩` something **real (objective)** or just a **tool (subjective)** for predictions?

---

## Two Main Perspectives

**1. Objective View (Real)**
- `|ψ⟩` represents something physically real in the universe.
- The quantum state exists independently of observation.

**2. Subjective View (Tool)**
- `|ψ⟩` is just a mathematical tool to predict measurement outcomes.
- It does not represent physical reality itself.

---

## Interpretations of Quantum Mechanics

**Copenhagen Interpretation:**
- `|ψ⟩` is **not real**, just a calculation tool.
- Reality only becomes definite upon measurement (collapse).
